Ranked!

-

49.9g of fat. Omega3 is where things start to fall off. For nuts, fat quality has to justify the calorie density.

-

21.6g of carbs. Fibre is doing a lot of the work here. For a nut, good fats have to justify the calorie density.

-

21.2g of protein. Bioavailability and amino acid quality are both strong. Protein is support here, not the whole argument.

-

Vitamin e at 171% daily value stands out most. That is major vitamin support.

-

Magnesium at 67% daily value, zinc at 28% daily value, iron at 21% daily value stand out most. That gives this food real mineral support.

-

Pros first: fairly satiating for the portion. Dense texture and fibre can help fullness. Easy to use as a practical wholefood snack. Convenient and shelf-stable enough to fit many routines. Contains useful polyphenols. Adds plant-compound value beyond the standard nutrient table.

-

The drawbacks next: contains some anti-nutrients. Phytates may reduce mineral absorption somewhat. Easy to overeat calories quickly. Energy density can add up fast in large handfuls. Not suitable for everyone. Tree nut allergy risk matters in practice.

-

Almonds has a genuinely strong overall case. The practical downsides are real, but they do not outweigh the upside. Best in small amounts where the fats and minerals matter more than sheer calories.
