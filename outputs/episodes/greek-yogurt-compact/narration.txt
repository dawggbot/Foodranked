Greek Yogurt ranked.

5g of fat. cholesterol, saturated fat, and polyunsaturated fat are doing most of the work. for dairy, the fat side can either add richness or drag the whole thing down.

3.9g of carbs. sugar, glycemic index, and starch are doing most of the work. for dairy, that is exactly the kind of thing you want.

9g of protein. bioavailability and amino acid quality are both strong. useful protein can rescue a lot of weaker traits here.

vitamin b12 at 22% daily value, vitamin a at 2% daily value stand out most. that is barely moving the needle.

calcium at 11% daily value, zinc at 5% daily value, magnesium at 3% daily value stand out most. useful, but still pretty limited.

on the plus side: fermented-food angle adds extra value because that gives it a practical benefit beyond the visible protein and fat numbers alone. quite filling for the calories because the protein density gives it strong practical satiety value. easy high-protein staple because very easy to build meals or snacks around.

now the downsides: saturated fat is not trivial because still much cleaner than weak dairy foods, but not ultra-low-fat. not suitable for everyone because lactose tolerance and dairy tolerance vary. healthiness can shift with toppings because sugary add-ins can change the practical outcome fast.

Greek Yogurt has a genuinely strong overall case. the practical downsides are real, but they do not outweigh the upside. best when the protein payoff or fermentation angle outweighs the fat and sodium tradeoffs.

S tier.
