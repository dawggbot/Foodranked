Tomato!

-

Ranked!

-

0.2g of fat. Omega 3 is doing a lot of the work here. For a vegetable, nutrient payoff is the whole point.

-

3.9g of carbs. Glycemic index, fibre, and sugar are doing most of the work. For vegetables, low downside is useful, but there still needs to be real payoff.

-

0.9g of protein. Bioavailability is one of the best parts of the protein story. Protein is not the main pitch here, but extra support still matters.

-

Vitamin c at 17% daily value stands out most. That is barely moving the needle.

-

Minerals are basically not part of the case here.

-

Pros first: good volume for the calories. That matters for satiety and general usefulness. Easy to build meals around. A practical plus because good foods need realistic use cases. Very low downside whole food. Vegetables win a lot simply by offering clean nutritional payoff.

-

The drawbacks next: energy density is very low. Good for some use cases, but not enough on its own. Usually needs pairing to make a full meal. Most vegetables are not complete by themselves. Rarely carries a meal by itself. That is normal here, but still part of the tradeoff.

-

Tomato does have a few real-world upsides. But the nutrition case is still weak and the downsides drag it down hard. Best only when you need a low-commitment add-on rather than a nutrient-dense anchor.

-

C tier.
