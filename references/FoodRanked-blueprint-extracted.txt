Apples ranked…

Fats:
Very low fat. Not a meaningful fat source.
All four are extremely low — saturated fat, omega-3, poly-unsaturated fat, and cholesterol are basically negligible.

Carbs:
High carb relative to its calories. Primarily a natural sugar source.
Sugar is the highest. Starch is basically zero. Fiber is moderate, and glucose index is low. So it’s sugar-dominant, but slow-digesting.

Proteins:
Extremely low. Not a protein source.
All four are very low — no collagen, zero essential amino acids, zero non-essentials, and zero bioavailability impact.

Micronutrients:
Strongest point is vitamin C. Potassium is decent for the calorie cost.
Very low in vitamin D and B12.
Most other vitamins and minerals are modest — nothing extreme.

Pros:
Low GI sugar + pectin fiber = stable energy, gut-friendly fermentation into short-chain fatty acids.
Polyphenols support vascular health and insulin sensitivity beyond what the vitamin panel suggests.
Very low energy density (~52 kcal/100g) makes it excellent for appetite control.

Cons:
Micronutrient density is modest. It’s not a “vitamin bomb,” just a gentle contributor.
Fructose dominant, in high quantities it can stress the liver if total diet is sugar-heavy.

broccoli - ranked!

Fats:Very low. Low omega 3. Everything else is negligible.

Carbs:
Low. Extremely low glucose index though. Low sugar is a bonus.

Protein:Very low. Some protein. But low quality.

Vitamins and minerals:Vitamin C and k is very high, basically 100% daily value. Completely lacking in vitamin D and B12. Everything else is mediocre. 

Pros:
Contains sulforaphane, a compound linked to antioxidant enzyme activation and potential cancer-protective effects.
Extremely high food volume for low calories.
The fiber plus glucosinolates (sulfur compounds) support gut microbiome.

Cons:
Can cause bloating and gas.
Overboiling destroys vitamin C and reduces sulforaphane formation.

Potatoes - ranked!

Fats:Completely low in all fats.

Carbs:
Mostly a starchy carb source. Super high glycemic index though.

Protein:The total protein is low, but the amino acid profile and bioavailability is better than people assume. 

Vitamins and minerals:Vitamin C is strong for a carb source. Everything else is lackluster other than potassium.

Pros:
Resistant starch formation after cooling feeds beneficial gut bacteria.
In controlled research, boiled potatoes ranked among the most filling foods per calorie.
Easy-digesting starch makes them excellent post-training fuel when rapid glycogen replenishment is useful.

Cons:
When exposed to light, potatoes produce solanine, a natural toxin.
Can promote overconsumption when hyper-processed.

oats - ranked!

Fats:Moderate fats with a fairly decent balance between all types of fat.

Carbs:
A full source of carbs. Mostly starch. High fiber. Minimal sugars.

Protein:A surprising source of protein. Even including the amino acid profiles. However, bioavailability limits this a lot. 

Vitamins and minerals:Quite mineral dense. Very little vitamins.

Pros:
High β-glucan fiber, which lowers LDL cholesterol and improves gut microbiome.
Extremely satiating. Fiber + starch slows stomach emptying.
Contains avenanthramides, oat-specific antioxidants that improve endothelial function and supports blood flow and anti-inflammatory signaling.

Cons:
Contains phytic acid, which reduces absorption of minerals like zinc and iron unless soaked or fermented.
β-glucans ferment strongly in the gut. can cause bloating.
Often contaminated with gluten during processing, unless labelled certified gluten-free. risky for certain people.

Uncooked white rice - ranked!

Fats:Low fat all round.

Carbs:
Huge carb source. Almost all starch. Slightly high glycemic index.

Protein:Protein is decent. None of it matters too much though.

Vitamins and minerals:Not very micronutrient dense. Only zinc is significant.

Pros:
Low fiber, low fat, and low anti-nutrients makes it one of the easiest carb sources on the gut.
Rice rarely triggers immune reactions compared with wheat, soy, or legumes.
Dry white rice can store for 10–30 years if sealed from oxygen. Few staple foods match that.

Cons:
Rice protein digestibility is decent, but lysine is very low, which limits its usefulness for muscle repair.
Rapid digestion can spike blood glucose unless paired with protein, fat, or fiber.
Rice absorbs more inorganic arsenic from soil than most crops. Rinsing and cooking in excess water reduces it.

Dry chickpeas - ranked!

Fats:Moderate fats. Ok overall.

Carbs:
Strong carb source. Very strong fiber and glycemic index too.

Protein:High protein. But low amino acids and bioavailability.

Vitamins and minerals:Small vitamin profile. Strong mineral profile though. Iron, magnesium, and zinc being the most noticeable.  

Pros:
The combo of fiber, resistant starch, and protein produces very slow digestion, controlling appetite and glucose spikes.
Chickpeas are extremely culinarily versatile. Few whole foods can transform into so many completely different dishes across cuisines.
Chickpeas enrich soil fertility, reducing the need for synthetic fertilizers.

Cons:
Contains anti-nutrients which reduce mineral absorption unless soaked or cooked well.
High raffinose and other fermentable carbs can cause gas or bloating.
Low in methionine, meaning chickpeas alone aren’t an optimal protein source for muscle repair.
